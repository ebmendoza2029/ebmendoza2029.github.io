What is Space?

We often refer to our expanding universe with one simple word: space. But where does space begin and, more importantly, what is it?

Definition:

Also known as the term 'cosmos', it refers to the entire physical universe as a coherent whole. The article universe describes humanity's developing awareness of all the items and phenomena that make up the cosmic system. Cosmology is a history of studying the cosmos as a whole.

Space is a nearly perfect vacuum, free of substance and at extremely low pressure. Sound does not travel over space because the molecules are not near enough together to transfer sound. Bits of gas, dust, and other stuff float about "emptier" portions of the cosmos, whereas planets, stars, and galaxies can be found in more congested environments.

From Earth's perspective, outer space is commonly regarded to begin roughly 62 miles (100 kilometers) above sea level at the Kármán line. This is an imagined border at an altitude where there isn't enough air to breathe or scatter light. As you approach this altitude, the sky begins to turn black because there aren't enough oxygen molecules to keep it blue.

Fun Facts about Space

  • The hottest planet in our solar system is Venus.
  • One million Earths could fit inside the Sun.
  • A full NASA space suit costs $12,000,000.
  • There are thousands of planets that we know of outside of our solar system.
  • Neutron stars can spin 600 times per second.
  • The largest known star is UY Scuti. It is 1700 times larger than the sun.
  • 99% of our solar system's mass is in the sun.
  • There may be a planet made out of diamonds.
  • A day on Venus is longer than a year on Venus.
